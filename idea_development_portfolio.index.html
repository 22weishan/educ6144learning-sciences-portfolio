Intro

Learning to See the Problem Differently

I came into this course as a language educator and tool designer with a problem I cared about but couldn't fully articulate. I had spent years teaching English listening and speaking to Chinese EFL students, and I was building ShadowingLab, a pronunciation practice tool, as my capstone project. I knew something was wrong with how spoken English was taught and learned in Chinese contexts. I just didn't have the theoretical language to explain what it was, or a clear direction for what a better alternative might look like. This portfolio documents how that changed.

My problem focus is pronunciation instruction in Chinese EFL contexts: specifically, how to shift the goal of spoken language learning away from accent elimination and toward intelligibility and identity affirmation, and what kinds of tools and environments can support that shift. This focus sharpened over the course of the semester through three interconnected ideas, each of which is documented here.

The first developed idea traces how my understanding of accent itself changed, from a deficit to be corrected toward a marker of linguistic identity that deserves to be preserved. The second traces how my vision of ShadowingLab changed, from a tool for individual practice toward an environment that could support collective knowledge building around spoken language learning. The rise-above synthesis connects these two ideas through the lens of productive failure and scaffolding design, arriving at a concrete design argument: that what Chinese EFL learners need is not more correction, but a learning environment designed to make failure productive.

The Make It Real section translates these ideas into a direct examination of ShadowingLab's design, showing where learning sciences principles are already embedded in the tool and where they point toward future design decisions. Throughout, I draw on course readings, my own prior knowledge from phonetics and language education, and the community discussions that shaped my thinking in ways I couldn't have anticipated at the start of the semester.

Idea 1

From Accent Elimination to Identity Navigation

Where the idea started

My interest in pronunciation instruction didn't begin in this course. It came from years of teaching English listening and speaking to Chinese EFL students, and from a phonetics research project I was working on in parallel this semester, investigating how shadowing affects intonation acquisition in Chinese learners.

What I had observed in Chinese EFL contexts was a systemic problem, and a complex one. It wasn't simply that pronunciation instruction was misguided. More often, it was that pronunciation instruction, along with the broader development of listening and spoken communication skills, was largely absent. English education in Chinese schools has historically been organized around exam preparation, with vocabulary, reading, and grammar taking center stage (Zheng, 2024). Speaking and listening, and certainly the phonological dimensions of spoken language, were treated as peripheral. Students could pass high-stakes exams without ever developing the ability to communicate comfortably in English, and without ever receiving meaningful guidance on pronunciation.

Within this neglect, accent remained an unexamined assumption. When pronunciation was addressed at all, the implicit standard was native-like speech. However, this goal was rarely made explicit, rarely scaffolded, and rarely questioned. The result was a kind of double invisibility: students received little instruction in pronunciation, and the instruction they did receive often left them feeling that any trace of a Chinese accent was something to be ashamed of, without giving them any realistic path toward changing it.

I came into this course with that observation already in place, but without a theoretical language to explain what was going wrong, or what a better alternative might look like.

How the idea developed

The first shift came in Week 3, when I engaged with the conceptual change literature. The distinction between treating prior knowledge as an obstacle to be cleared away versus treating it as a resource to be built upon struck me as directly relevant to pronunciation instruction (diSessa, 2022). In many learners and teachers' minds, L1-shaped phonological intuitions are positioned as errors, something to be corrected and eventually replaced. But the knowledge-in-pieces perspective suggests a different framing: those intuitions are not random noise. They are structured, meaningful, and deeply connected to how learners have been making sense of sound their entire lives. The question isn't how to eliminate them, but how to work with them.

This reframing was useful, but it still felt incomplete. It addressed the cognitive dimension of pronunciation learning, but it didn't account for something I had observed repeatedly in my teaching: the emotional and identity-related dimension. Students weren't just struggling phonologically. They were struggling with who they were allowed to be when speaking English.

The more significant shift came in Week 13, when I read Bang and Medin's (2010) work on supporting students to navigate multiple epistemologies in science education. Their argument is that students from non-dominant cultural backgrounds are often pressured to abandon their own ways of knowing in order to succeed academically, and that good educational design should instead support navigation between epistemological systems rather than replacement of one by another. Reading this, I felt something click into place. The parallel to pronunciation instruction was immediate and striking.

In Chinese EFL contexts, accent is treated as a deficit, a sign that the learner hasn't yet succeeded in replacing their L1-influenced phonological system with a target-language one. But if we take Bang and Medin seriously, accent is not evidence of failure. It is evidence of navigation, of a learner moving between two linguistic and cultural systems simultaneously. A Chinese-accented English speaker is not failing to reach a native-speaker target. They are constructing a communicative identity that spans two languages.

This gave me a way to articulate what I had been observing in my teaching but couldn't fully name: the problem with accent elimination as a pedagogical goal isn't just that it's cognitively ineffective. It's that it asks learners to treat their own linguistic identity as something to be ashamed of and discarded.

Where the idea landed

Drawing on this, and on the applied linguistics literature I had encountered through my phonetics work, particularly the intelligibility principle and the arguments in Pronunciation Fundamentals (Thomson & Derwing, 2015) that accent and comprehensibility are independent, I arrived at a reformulated goal for pronunciation instruction: intelligibility with identity preservation.

The target is not accent-free speech. The target is speech that can be understood across contexts, while allowing learners to retain the markers of their linguistic and cultural identity. Accent becomes a bridge between two linguistic and cultural systems, not a problem to be solved.

This reframing also directly shaped how I think about the design of ShadowingLab. Shadowing practice, by its nature, involves close imitation of a model speaker. Without explicit guidance, learners can easily interpret this as a demand for perfect replication, which reintroduces the same anxiety and shame that accent-elimination framing produces. One concrete design decision that came out of this idea is that ShadowingLab's feedback and instructional language needs to explicitly communicate that the goal is not perfect imitation. Learners should be guided to focus on the features of speech that affect intelligibility, such as intonation patterns, rhythm, and stress, while being reassured that retaining some phonological traces of their L1 is not a failure. The accent is part of who they are. The practice is about communication, not replacement.

This was a small but meaningful shift in how I think about tool design: the framing and language of a learning environment carries its own pedagogical assumptions, and those assumptions need to be made conscious and intentional.

Idea 2

From Individual Practice to Knowledge-Building Community

Where the idea started

ShadowingLab began as an individual practice tool. The design logic was straightforward: a learner works through a structured shadowing session, receives feedback on their pronunciation, and improves over time through repeated practice. This design reflected a broader reality I had observed in Chinese EFL contexts: pronunciation and spoken language practice has traditionally been a solitary activity, and often an absent one. In most classroom settings, there is no structured space for learners to discuss their experiences with intonation or accent, to share what they notice, or to learn from each other's developing understanding of spoken English. My initial design didn't challenge that norm. It worked within it.

This assumption went unexamined until I started engaging with the knowledge building literature in Week 2, and more importantly, until I started experiencing what community-based learning actually felt like from the inside.

How the idea developed

The theoretical shift came early. Scardamalia and Bereiter's (2022) knowledge building principles, particularly the ideas of community knowledge and improvable ideas, introduced me to a vision of learning that I hadn't seriously considered in the context of language education. The core claim, that knowledge isn't just something individuals acquire but something communities collectively advance, felt almost obvious in retrospect, but it hadn't been part of how I thought about pronunciation learning at all.

What made this theoretical shift real, though, was my own experience on Perusall and Wonderbits throughout the semester. I found myself encountering perspectives and connections I would not have produced on my own. One moment that stayed with me came from a classmate's comment during Week 8 discussions. Hanson made the point that shifting the analytic stance from measuring a learner's deviation from an expert template toward understanding the learner's own knowledge organization was deeply aligned with constructivist learning theory, and that teaching depends on diagnosing learners' current conceptions rather than just their distance from the correct answer. This directly connected to something I had written in my own Perusall annotation that week, where I had drawn a parallel to the history of language assessment: how English assessment had evolved from testing discrete, decontextualized points like individual phoneme discrimination toward more authentic, task-based assessment that reflects real communicative ability.

Reading Hanson's comment alongside my own made something visible that I hadn't fully articulated before: both observations were pointing at the same underlying problem. Whether in research methodology or in language assessment, the instinct to measure learners against an external standard, rather than to understand their own developing system, produces a distorted picture of what learning actually looks like. And that distortion has consequences for design.

The community discussion helped me see a connection I hadn't made on my own.

Where the idea landed

This experience of benefiting from community discussion became a design argument. If I was gaining insight from the collective thinking of my peers in ways I couldn't replicate alone, then there was no reason to assume pronunciation learners wouldn't benefit from the same kind of environment.

Pronunciation and spoken language practice has traditionally been the most isolated dimension of language learning. Learners rarely have structured opportunities to discuss their experiences with intonation and accent the way they might discuss vocabulary or reading. There is no obvious platform where people who are specifically trying to develop their spoken English can share observations, ask questions, and learn from each other's noticing.

This became the rationale for adding a community forum to ShadowingLab. The goal isn't just to create a space for social interaction. Drawing on the knowledge building principle that ideas should feed into further development rather than remain isolated (Scardamalia & Bereiter, 2022), the forum is designed as a space where learners can make their noticing visible, share observations about specific phonological features, and collectively advance their understanding of what intelligible, confident spoken English sounds and feels like. The learner's experience of shadowing, what they notice, what confuses them, what suddenly clicks, becomes an epistemic object that others can engage with, build on, and respond to.

The design decision was small in practice but significant in principle: ShadowingLab is no longer just a tool for individual practice. It is an environment for collective knowledge building around spoken language learning.

Rise-Above

Designing for Productive Failure in Pronunciation Learning

The tension I kept returning to

Across several weeks of this course, I kept encountering a tension that felt directly relevant to my problem focus, but that I couldn't quite resolve on my own. On one hand, the literature was clear that struggle, uncertainty, and even failure are not obstacles to learning but conditions for it. On the other hand, everything I had observed about Chinese EFL learners and spoken English pointed to a situation where the fear of failure was so pervasive that many learners simply stopped trying. The question I couldn't get past was: if productive failure is a powerful mechanism for learning, why does failure in pronunciation practice so often produce the opposite effect?

Productive failure and its conditions

Kapur and Bielaczyc's (2012) work on productive failure, which I engaged with in Week 4, gave me a useful starting point. The core argument is that allowing learners to struggle with a problem before receiving formal instruction, even when that struggle results in incorrect or incomplete solutions, activates prior knowledge, surfaces key conceptual features, and prepares learners for deeper understanding. The short-term cost of apparent failure produces long-term learning gains that direct instruction alone cannot achieve.

What struck me about this framework was not just the argument itself, but the conditions it depends on. Productive failure doesn't happen automatically. It requires a learning environment that is designed to make failure safe, meaningful, and productive. In Kapur and Bielaczyc's studies, learners worked collaboratively, generated multiple solution attempts, and received structured consolidation afterward. The failure was productive because the environment was designed to make it so.

This raised an immediate question for pronunciation learning: what would it take to design an environment where learners could fail productively at spoken English, rather than just failing in ways that increased their anxiety and silenced them further?

Scaffolding as the missing design element

The Week 9 readings and facilitation on scaffolding gave me the design language I needed to answer that question. The distinction between adaptive and adaptable scaffolding was particularly illuminating (Kollar, Wecker, & Fischer, 2018). Adaptive scaffolding adjusts to the learner's current state, providing more or less support depending on where the learner is in their development. Adaptable scaffolding goes further: it is designed to be modified, faded, and eventually removed as the learner grows. Both concepts point toward a vision of support that is dynamic rather than fixed, responsive rather than prescriptive.

Applied to pronunciation learning, this suggested something specific. The problem with traditional pronunciation instruction in Chinese EFL contexts isn't just that it sets the wrong goal. It's also that the scaffolding structure is either absent or rigid. Learners are either left to practice alone without support, or they are given feedback that functions as correction rather than as a scaffold toward greater communicative confidence. Neither approach creates the conditions for productive failure.

The synthesis

Bringing these threads together, I arrived at an insight that felt new to me, one that I don't think I could have reached by staying within any single framework. The anxiety and silence that characterize many Chinese EFL learners' relationship with spoken English is not primarily a motivation problem or a phonological problem. It is a design problem. The learning environments these students inhabit have not been designed to make failure productive. There is no scaffold that normalizes the experience of imperfect speech, no structure that helps learners distinguish between failures that matter for intelligibility and failures that don't, and no community in which the iterative, uncertain process of developing a spoken voice can be shared and supported.

The implication for ShadowingLab is concrete. Designing for productive failure in pronunciation learning means building in three things that are currently missing from most EFL spoken language environments: explicit framing that positions imperfect speech as a normal and expected part of the learning process, adaptive feedback that helps learners focus on intelligibility-relevant features rather than accent elimination, and a community space where the experience of struggle can be made visible and shared. Together, these elements create the conditions under which failure stops being threatening and starts being productive.

This synthesis also clarified something about the relationship between my two developed ideas. Developed Idea 1 addressed the question of what the goal of pronunciation instruction should be. Developed Idea 2 addressed the question of what kind of social environment supports that goal. This rise-above adds a third layer: what the design of the learning experience itself needs to look like in order to make the goal reachable.

Make It Real

ShadowingLab as a Learning Sciences-Grounded Design

ShadowingLab is a web-based shadowing practice tool I have been building this semester as my capstone project. Its purpose is to help Chinese EFL high school students develop the bottom-up processing skills that years of exam-oriented listening instruction have left underdeveloped (Zheng, 2024). Students who can pass the Gaokao listening test but still cannot follow a real conversation are not lacking in effort. They are lacking in the right kind of practice, and in the cognitive tools to know what to pay attention to when they practice.

What I want to do here is not describe the tool in technical terms, but explain how the ideas I developed in this course are woven into its design, and in some cases, how they changed the design in ways I hadn't anticipated when I started.

Noticing as the core mechanism

The five-phase session structure at the heart of ShadowingLab grows directly out of Schmidt's (1990) Noticing Hypothesis, which I had encountered before this course, but understood differently after engaging with the learning sciences literature. The idea that conscious attention is a necessary condition for second language acquisition had always seemed to me primarily a cognitive claim: learners need to notice the gap between their own production and the target. What this course added was a design implication I hadn't fully worked out. Noticing needs to be scaffolded, not just prompted. It isn't enough to tell learners to pay attention. The environment needs to structure their attention in ways that make noticing possible and capture what they notice so it can feed into future learning.

This is why Phase 4 of the session, where learners listen to their own recording alongside the original and log tagged notices by phonological category, is designed the way it is. The notice log isn't a record-keeping exercise. It is an epistemic artifact in the knowledge building sense: something the learner creates that externalizes their current understanding and makes it available for reflection, revision, and connection to future sessions.

Scaffolding that fades

The scaffolding design in ShadowingLab draws on the distinction between adaptive and adaptable scaffolding that I encountered in Week 9 (Kollar, Wecker, & Fischer, 2018). The five-phase structure itself is a form of process scaffolding: it guides learners through a sequence they wouldn't naturally follow on their own, reducing the cognitive demands of knowing what to do next so they can focus on the perceptual work of shadowing. But the design also includes an Adaptive Fading mechanism in the Progress dashboard: phonological features that have been absent from a learner's notice log for five consecutive sessions are marked as mastered and gradually withdrawn from the annotation layer. The scaffold adjusts to the learner's developing competence rather than remaining fixed at the level of a novice indefinitely.

What this course helped me see more clearly is that scaffolding is not just a feature to be added to a tool. It is a relationship between the tool's structure and the learner's current zone of proximal development. A scaffold that doesn't respond to where the learner actually is isn't scaffolding. It's just structure.

Designing as an iterative process

Week 11's discussion of Design-Based Research also shaped how I think about ShadowingLab as a design project, not just as a product. The DBR framework positions design not as a linear process from idea to implementation, but as an iterative cycle of grounding, conjecturing, testing, and reflecting (Puntambekar, 2018). Each iteration produces not just a refined artifact but new knowledge about the relationship between the design and the learning it is meant to support.

Looking back at ShadowingLab's development across this semester, from v1 to the current v5, I can see this logic at work. Early versions were grounded primarily in the shadowing literature and cognitive load theory. Each iteration introduced new design conjectures: that phonological annotations would reduce extraneous load, that a structured notice log would support conscious noticing, that adaptive fading would make scaffolding more responsive to individual learners. The research mode in ShadowingLab began as a practical bridge between two parallel projects this semester: connecting the tool to a phonetics study I was running on the effects of shadowing on intonation acquisition. But as the design developed, the purpose expanded. The research mode now serves a longer-term aim: to collect acoustic data that can pinpoint the specific effects of shadowing on spoken English, and eventually to surface that evidence within the tool itself, giving learners concrete, measurable feedback on how their pronunciation is changing over time. What DBR gave me was a framework for understanding this kind of iterative, evidence-seeking design not as a detour from building the product, but as the process through which the product becomes more grounded and more useful.

Reframing the goal: intelligibility over accent

This is perhaps the most significant design implication that came directly from the ideas I developed in this course. ShadowingLab's original design philosophy was grounded in noticing and cognitive load, but it didn't explicitly address what learners should be trying to achieve when they shadow. The implicit assumption was that the goal was accurate imitation of native-speaker speech.

Developing Idea 1 in this course forced me to examine that assumption. If accent and intelligibility are not the same thing, as Thomson and Derwing (2015) argue and as Bang and Medin's (2010) framework helped me see more clearly, then a tool that positions perfect native-speaker imitation as the target is reproducing the same deficit framing that makes so many Chinese EFL learners reluctant to speak in the first place. The phonological annotations in ShadowingLab mark features like stress, linking, and intonation, not because learners need to eliminate every trace of their L1 phonology, but because these are the features that most directly affect whether spoken English is understood across contexts.

This reframing needs to be explicit in the tool itself. A design decision that came out of this course is that ShadowingLab's instructional language and feedback framing should make clear to learners that the goal is communicative intelligibility, not accent-free speech. The accent is part of who they are. The practice is about developing the phonological awareness and control that allows them to be understood, not about becoming someone else.

The community dimension: a design aspiration

The one significant gap in ShadowingLab's current design, and one I am now more aware of because of this course, is the absence of any social or community dimension. The tool is built for individual practice. There is no space for learners to share what they are noticing, to discuss what feels difficult, or to learn from each other's developing understanding of spoken English.

Engaging with the knowledge building literature and experiencing firsthand what community discussion made possible in this course, through Perusall annotations and Wonderbits contributions, convinced me that this gap matters. Pronunciation and spoken language practice is already the most isolated dimension of language learning for most Chinese EFL students. A tool that replicates that isolation misses an opportunity.

Adding a community forum to ShadowingLab is technically complex and beyond the scope of what I can implement this semester. But it is now a clear design aspiration rather than an afterthought: a space where learners can make their noticing public, share observations about specific phonological features, and collectively build understanding of what intelligible, confident spoken English sounds and feels like. The notice log that currently exists as a private record could, in a future version, become a shared epistemic artifact that the community can engage with and build on.
